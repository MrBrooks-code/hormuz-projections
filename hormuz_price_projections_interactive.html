What This Is

On February 28, 2026, US and Israeli strikes on Iranian nuclear and military infrastructure (Operation Epic Fury) triggered the closure of the Strait of Hormuz — the narrow waterway through which approximately 21 million barrels of oil per day transit, representing roughly 20% of global supply. Tanker traffic dropped from a normal 24 vessels/day to near zero within 72 hours. We are now on Day 14. Iran's new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei declared the strait will stay closed as a "tool of pressure." The US Navy has designated the strait a "kill box" — escort operations are suspended. Oman's Mina Al Fahal terminal, the last viable bypass route, has been evacuated. Trump stated he has "plenty of time," signaling tolerance for extended conflict. Wall Street is now pricing a long war.

This report models the downstream effect on US consumer prices across 17 goods and services categories — from gasoline and groceries to appliances and airfare — as a function of how long the disruption persists. It is built on empirical pass-through research from past oil shocks and answers the question: if the strait stays closed for N weeks, what does a trip to the grocery store cost?

How To Use It

Use the duration slider below to set the number of weeks the Hormuz closure persists. All prices update instantly. Each item has its own built-in lag — gasoline responds within days, manufactured goods take 5–6 weeks before retail prices move — so the model reflects real supply chain timing, not a simultaneous reprice. Slider is set to current week (2).

The snapshot cards give a quick read on headline impacts. The full price table shows every item's trajectory. The Methodology panel at the bottom documents every formula, parameter, and source citation so the projections can be interrogated or challenged.

01 — Model Overview

This model projects consumer prices under a sustained Strait of Hormuz disruption scenario. It does not use a live oil price feed — instead it maps crisis duration in weeks directly to pass-through percentages derived from empirical research on past oil shock events (1973 embargo, 1979 Iranian revolution, 2022 Ukraine war). The core insight is that velocity of disruption matters more than absolute price: supply chains run on predictability, and a rapid sustained shock breaks contracts and pricing assumptions before adaptation can occur.

Each item is modeled independently with its own lag (weeks before prices begin moving), anchor percentages at weeks 4 and 13, and a theoretical maximum. The slider controls week, and all prices recalculate live.

02 — What Week 4 and Week 13 Actually Mean

These two milestones are not arbitrary — they map to documented physical thresholds in supply chain behavior observed across every major oil shock in the empirical record.

Week 4 — Point of No Return ≈ Mar 28, 2026
By week 4, three irreversible thresholds have triggered simultaneously. (1) Refinery feedstock buffers (~16 days of crude on hand) are exhausted — refineries begin cutting run rates regardless of price. (2) Commercial inventory erodes below the minimum forward-contract thresholds logistics companies need to keep quoting freight. (3) Trade finance seizes — banks cannot price the risk of issuing Letters of Credit when oil price volatility exceeds their risk models, so ships stop sailing even if the physical threat is reduced. These three events compound: lower refinery output → tighter commercial stocks → higher freight volatility → more LC withdrawals. The cascade becomes self-reinforcing. Week 4 is called the Point of No Return because even a strait reopening at this stage cannot prevent the inflation from propagating — the supply chain disruption has already been transmitted upstream into pricing assumptions, contract renegotiations, and inventory drawdown decisions that take months to unwind.
Week 13 — Full Pass-Through Complete ≈ May 30, 2026
Week 13 (~3 months) is the empirically documented horizon at which oil price shocks have fully propagated through all layers of the consumer price chain in past events. This is not a model assumption — it is the observed endpoint from the 1973 embargo, 1979 Iranian revolution, and 2022 Ukraine war datasets. The propagation works in layers: crude oil reprices within days → refined fuel within 1–2 weeks → food transport and cold chain within 3–6 weeks → packaged goods (petroleum-derived packaging) within 6–10 weeks → restaurant menus and manufactured goods within 8–13 weeks. By week 13, every category has absorbed its first-round shock. After week 13, the model enters structural embedding — the phase where wage negotiations, long-term supply contracts, and rent resets begin incorporating the new price level as the baseline, making inflation sticky and self-perpetuating regardless of what oil does next. At the current pace of the 2026 crisis, the week 13 consumer price scenario is no longer a tail risk — it is the baseline regardless of resolution timing, because the price signal has already transmitted far enough into supply chains that most of the pass-through is locked in.
03 — Pass-Through Formula

The core function passThrough(week, w4pct, w13pct, maxpct, lag) returns the percentage price increase above baseline. It operates in three segments based on effectiveWeek = week − lag:

Segment 1 — Early Ramp  effectiveWeek ∈ [0, w4]
passThrough = (effectiveWeek / w4) × w4pct
Linear. Prices begin moving immediately once lag expires, ramping to the week-4 anchor. Chosen because early pass-through in empirical data is approximately linear before supply chain cascades activate.
Segment 2 — Cascade Acceleration  effectiveWeek ∈ (w4, w13]
t = (effectiveWeek − w4) / (w13 − w4)
passThrough = w4pct + (w13pct − w4pct) × t0.75
Power curve with exponent 0.75 (slightly concave). Prices accelerate faster than linear as cascade effects compound — each upstream repricing triggers downstream repricing. Exponent calibrated against UCLA Anderson supply chain indirect effect data showing ~60–70% of total 13-week pass-through occurring in weeks 5–10.
Segment 3 — Structural Embedding  effectiveWeek ∈ (w13, 52]
absoluteMax = maxpct × 1.5
t = (effectiveWeek − w13) / (52 − w13)
logT = ln(1 + 9t) / ln(10)    [normalized log scale]
passThrough = w13pct + (absoluteMax − w13pct) × logT
Logarithmic growth reflecting decelerating but sustained price pressure. absoluteMax = maxpct × 1.5 captures second-round effects: a year-long disruption embeds price increases 50% higher than a 13-week shock peak because wage adjustments, long-term contract resets, and embedded inflation expectations compound. The log shape (fast early gains, decelerating approach to ceiling) matches historical long-shock data from the 1973 embargo (18-month echo) and 2022 Ukraine energy shock.
Final Price Calculation
projectedPrice = baselinePrice × (1 + passThrough / 100)
Simple multiplicative application. No compounding between weeks — the pass-through percentage is always relative to the Jan 2026 baseline, not the prior week's price.

Upstream vs downstream repricing lag analysis. Source for manufactured goods lag parameters (5–8 weeks) and the finding that ~75% of indirect oil price effects appear within 6 months for upstream industries.
IMF Working Paper WP/22/173
Oil shock pass-through across consumer categories. Calibration source for food (25–40% at 13 weeks) and services categories. Used to differentiate transport-dependent vs energy-intensive sectors.

Weekly granularity pass-through data. Primary source for gasoline segment anchors (37–50% confirmed at 13 weeks). Power curve exponent (0.75) calibrated against this weekly progression data.

06 — Known Limitations & Assumptions
⚠ No live oil price feed
Duration maps to pass-through directly. Actual WTI price matters — a week-8 crisis at $85/bbl differs from one at $115/bbl. This model assumes a "typical" price trajectory for Hormuz closure.
⚠ US-centric only
Pass-through rates are calibrated for the US consumer market. Other countries have different energy tax structures, subsidy programs, and import dependencies that would yield different curves.
⚠ No demand destruction modeled
At extreme price levels, consumers substitute or forgo purchases (demand destruction), which dampens real price increases. This model does not model substitution effects — projections at week 26+ may be upper bounds.
⚠ No policy intervention modeled
SPR releases, price controls, rationing, or emergency legislation could suppress prices significantly. The 2022 US SPR release demonstrably moderated gasoline prices. No government intervention is assumed.
⚠ Concurrent disruptions not modeled
A Hormuz closure of this magnitude would likely trigger equity market declines, credit tightening, and potential bank stress — all of which feed into consumer prices through separate channels not captured here.
ℹ The "$3/day" Oil Price Rule
Some analysts predicted crude would rise ~$3/day while the strait remains closed. Actual tracking: $66 WTI on Feb 28 → $97 on Mar 13 = $31 in 13 days = ~$2.38/day — close but slightly below the rule. This model does not use a per-day oil price mechanism. It maps weeks directly to empirical consumer pass-through percentages. The $3/day rule, if extrapolated linearly to week 13 (91 days), implies WTI of ~$340 — clearly non-physical. In practice, the rule applies to the early-crisis surge only; demand destruction, SPR releases, and recession fear create natural price ceilings. Our week 13 gasoline assumption (65%) implies a WTI-equivalent of roughly $135–145, consistent with the KPMG ($130+) and Goldman Sachs ($110 extreme) forecasts.
⚠ Segment 3 absoluteMax is theoretical
The ×1.5 multiplier is calibrated from 1973 analog data. There is no modern 52-week Hormuz closure in the empirical record — this segment is extrapolated, not directly observed.
